# The Business Case for VMI — Hidden Costs Made Visible

> *"The only business case that works is the one the customer builds themselves."*

---

## The Approach

The business case was not presented to customers. It was handed to them.

Rather than arriving with pre-built numbers, we gave customers an open input tool — an interactive ROI calculator where they entered their own operational data:

- Number of order lines per week
- Current hourly wage rates for the people handling procurement
- Estimated walk & wait time per day per employee
- Number of active suppliers in the indirect category
- Current consumption levels for key item groups

The model then calculated their specific hidden costs — and produced a personalised ROI estimate based entirely on their own numbers.

The result: customers could not argue against their own figures. The conversation shifted from *"convince me"* to *"how soon can we start."*

**That is the only business case that actually works.**

---

## The Hidden Cost Framework

Most organisations evaluate VMI on purchase price alone. That is the wrong number.

### 1. Invoice Processing — DKK ~1,000 per order

In a high-wage country like Denmark, the fully loaded cost of processing a single purchase order and its associated invoice is typically **DKK 1,000** — when all steps are counted honestly:

- Order creation and internal approval
- Goods receipt confirmation
- Invoice receipt and validation
- 3-way matching (PO / delivery / invoice)
- Bookkeeping and archiving
- Error correction and supplier follow-up

With VMI, this entire process is replaced by **one consolidated weekly invoice per site.**

A customer with 200 individual order lines per week:

> **200 lines × DKK 1,000 = DKK 200,000 hidden cost — per week — eliminated.**

---

### 2. Walk & Wait Time

Every minute a skilled employee spends:
- Walking to find stock
- Waiting for a delivery
- Searching for a missing item
- Chasing a supplier for an overdue order

...is a direct, measurable productivity loss.

In Danish industrial environments — production lines, workshops, offshore facilities — the fully loaded hourly cost for the people affected is typically **DKK 400–600**.

The calculator asked customers to estimate this themselves. The numbers were always larger than they expected.

VMI ensures the right item is in the right place at the right time. The search stops. The wait stops.

---

### 3. Consumption Reduction — typically 15–25%

When items are freely accessible without control or visibility, overconsumption follows naturally. People take extra "just in case". Items disappear without being recorded. No one notices until stock runs out.

Controlled access via RFID — implemented in both vending automater and StockMaster — changes behaviour immediately and measurably.

Experience from implementations at **Lemvigh-Müller** and **Sanistål/Ahlsell** shows consistent consumption reductions of **15–25%** from access control and visibility alone.

No process redesign required. No culture change programme. Just accountability built into the system.

---

### 4. Supplier Consolidation

VMI concentrates indirect purchasing with one supplier across multiple categories:

- Consumables
- PPE and safety equipment
- Tools and hand tools
- Spare parts and maintenance items
- Calibration-dependent instruments

The effect is compounding:
- Fewer suppliers to manage, onboard and audit
- Higher volume per supplier — stronger negotiating position
- Reduced procurement overhead across the entire indirect spend portfolio
- One invoice. One relationship. One point of accountability.

---

### 5. Asset Management — Rental and Calibration

The same VMI infrastructure managed two additional asset categories:

**Rental items**
Tools and equipment lent to employees or contractors — tracked by who has what, since when, and when it is due back. Automatic reminders. Dispute resolution based on data, not memory.

**Calibrated instruments**
Measuring instruments requiring periodic calibration — tracked by serial number, last calibration date, next due date, and certificate status.

All data captured. All data utilised. Implemented and live.

This extends VMI from a replenishment system to a **complete asset lifecycle platform.**

## The ROI Summary Table

| Cost driver | Without VMI | With VMI |
|---|---|---|
| Invoice processing | DKK ~1,000 per order line | 1 consolidated invoice per site per week |
| Walk & wait time | Untracked, uncontrolled | Eliminated |
| Consumption | Baseline | 15–25% reduction |
| Supplier complexity | Multiple vendors, high overhead | Consolidated — one supplier, one invoice |
| Asset tracking | Manual or absent | Automated, documented, auditable |
| Calibration management | Calendar reminders or forgotten | System-tracked, certificate-documented |

## Why the Interactive Approach Worked

Pre-built business cases fail for one reason: the customer can always question your assumptions.

*"Your DKK 1,000 per invoice — that's not what it costs us."*
*"Our walk & wait time isn't that high."*
*"We already have some consolidation."*

When customers enter their own numbers, those objections disappear. The model becomes a mirror — and mirrors are hard to argue with.

The conversation stops being about whether VMI is worth it. It becomes about when to start and where to begin.
